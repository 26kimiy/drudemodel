Optical fit: measuring the electrons instead of assuming them

Purpose: this page is here to understand the Drude model better, not to analyze attenuation. The 100 GHz loss numbers live in tab 2.

Tab 1 got the electron numbers by assuming things about the metal. Here we get them a second way, by measuring: we take real data on how each metal responds to light and tune the Drude model until it matches. Then we compare. Where the assumption was right the two agree; where it was wrong they do not.

The one idea on this page

ωp (the plasma frequency) is basically how many electrons are free to move. Tab 1 never measured it; it assumed every atom donates one free electron of ordinary weight, and counted atoms. Here we measure it from light. For silver and gold the assumption turns out right, so the two ways agree (about 9 eV). For copper it is wrong: the measurement (about 7.5 eV) lands well below the assumed count (10.8 eV), because copper's inner d-electrons get in the way and fewer electrons actually act free. That gap is the whole point of this page.

So why bother?

Two reasons. First, it is the honest way to pin ωp down, because it comes from real measurements instead of an assumption. Second, it tells us exactly when the simple model from tab 1 can be trusted (silver and gold) and when it cannot (copper). As a bonus it reveals the bound-electron background ε∞, which tab 1 just set to 1.

Your three knobs feed these equations, which draw the curves below (ε1 is the real part of ε):

ε(ω) = ε∞ − ωp2ω2 + iγω  →  n = √|ε| + ε12  ·  k = √|ε| − ε12

Misfit:  how far the lines are from the dots, on average. Lower is better; the best fit makes it as small as it can go.

What to notice: ωp slides the whole response left and right, γ controls how strongly the metal absorbs (the k curve), and ε∞ nudges the high-frequency end. There is exactly one combination that makes the lines hug the dots, and that combination is the metal's true Drude numbers. Our computer found it automatically by minimizing the misfit; the sliders just let you feel what it was doing.

Data sources: copper and gold use Ordal's far-infrared measurements [1]; silver has no Ordal set in the database, so it uses Johnson and Christy [2], an independent source. All pulled from [3].

About the γ row: ωp is the solid result and ours matches Ordal within about 5%. γ is softer and depends on which frequency you probe. Ordal's γ is essentially the value that reproduces the DC conductivity, while ours comes from the optical data, and electrons bump a little more often at higher frequency, so our γ runs slightly larger. Two honest numbers for two situations, not an error.

What to take away

  1. The method works. Our fit to real data recovers Ordal's published plasma frequencies (Cu 7.75 vs 7.39, Ag 9.17 vs 9.01, Au 8.94 vs 9.03 eV).
  2. Copper is the odd one out. Silver's and gold's assumed ωp (tab 1) matches the measured one, so the simple count works for them. Copper's assumed value (10.8) is about 40% too high, because its d-electrons leave fewer electrons acting free than the count assumes.
  3. Bound electrons appear as ε∞ near 4 for silver and gold, the inner electrons tab 1 ignored.

Bottom line: the optical fit is the trustworthy source for ωp. It does not replace tab 1 for our 100 GHz loss work, because there we need the DC conductivity, which the DC route nails. At 100 GHz the assumed and measured routes give almost the same conductivity; they only pull apart in the infrared, so the simple DC numbers in tabs 1 and 2 are the right ones there.
